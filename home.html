Iterative Design

Overview

In this project, I coupled my love for succulents with my interest in analytics to perform an A/B test and statistical analysis to gather insight into two different UI designs for an online succulent store. At this succulent store, every user must place at least $150 worth of plants into their shopping cart.

Developing Design B

To start this process, I needed to alter a UI design in a way that could potentially affect some of the metrics being tracked on the site. Hypotheses

Before I could continue in evaluating these changes through an A/B test, I needed to determine my expectations by creating hypotheses. In this particular test, I wanted to gain insight into the effects of these changes on two metrics:

1. Return Rate Hypotheses

  • Context: In design A, the user cannot see the total cost of their cart until they navigate to the checkout page. Because the user's goal is to obtain at least $150 in plants, I made the following hypotheses.
  • Alternative Hypothesis: If users can check the total dollar amount in their carts on the shopping page, then the return rate will decrease.
  • Null Hypothesis: If users can check the total dollar amount in their carts onthe shopping page, then the return rate will either remain the same or decrease.

2. Time to Completion Hypotheses

  • Context: In design A, the user must scroll to the bottom of the page to view all of the succulents and the checkout button.
  • Alternative Hypothesis: If the user can see all of the products and the checkout button without needing to scroll, the time to completion will decrease.
  • Null Hypothesis: If the user can see all of the products and the checkout button without needing to scroll, the time to completion will either increase or remain the same.

Data Collection

To collect data for the A/B test, I asked my section classmates, friends, and family to navigate the online store. Upon arrival to the store, either design A or B was displayed to the user. Each user was also given a task-- to add at least $150 worth of plants to the shopping cart.

I ended the test with 23 unique users. 12 used A and 11 used B.

Takeaways

I was a bit surprised by the results of the tests, as I was expecting them to be more significant; however there were certain that I think affected the results. The first being that many of my users were using the page repetitively, and they knew the goals and purpose of the assignment. In the future, I would cast a wider, more diverse net. I also think the content of checkout page played a larger role in return rate than either A or B. Users who enter the checkout page are only presented with one option "go back to shopping." I think this was the cause for signifiantly more returns, as a large portion of users who returned didn't actually add any additional items to their carts. In the future, I would be interested in running an A/B test on the checkout page!
